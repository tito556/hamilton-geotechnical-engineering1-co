Many earthworks contractors in the Waikato region still rely on widely spaced boreholes alone, then face costly delays when hidden basalt flows or peat lenses appear between the investigation points. In Hamilton, where the subsoil shifts from river silts to weathered greywacke within tens of metres, that gamble is unnecessary. Seismic tomography builds a continuous velocity model of the ground, flagging transitions that discrete drilling often misses. The method works by measuring P-wave and S-wave travel times along multiple ray paths, then inverting the data to produce a 2D or 3D stiffness profile. For large greenfield subdivisions east of the city, combining the tomography grid with targeted test pits helps calibrate seismic velocities against visual soil descriptions, giving the earthworks team a calibrated ground model before a single scraper moves.

A continuous velocity cross-section removes the interpolation risk that comes with borehole-only investigations, especially in Hamilton's variable alluvial and volcanic terrain.

Methodology applied in Hamilton

Under the NZGS guidelines for site investigation, seismic methods are recommended when the project demands a continuous image of the rockhead or when karst features are suspected. The Hamilton basin presents both challenges: buried river channels cut into the Te Kuiti Group limestone, and cavities can develop where acidic groundwater circulates. Refraction tomography excels at mapping the top of competent rock, which is essential for setting pile toe levels or planning excavation support. Reflection profiling, using higher-frequency sources, resolves finer layering within the overburden, such as the interface between alluvial sands and the underlying pumiceous silts. When the objective is to position a deep sewer alignment under the Waikato River floodplain, the reflection data feed directly into the slope stability analysis for the trench design, ensuring the benching geometry accounts for the actual soil stratigraphy rather than an interpolated guess. The field crew typically lays out 24 to 48 geophones with a sledgehammer or weight-drop source, and the entire spread can be redeployed quickly to cover long linear corridors.

Typical technical challenges in Hamilton

Hamilton's urban expansion has progressively moved onto land that earlier generations avoided: drained peat bogs near Rototuna, gullies filled with uncontrolled ash and demolition rubble, and terraces underlain by deeply weathered ignimbrite. The 2010s development boom saw several subdivisions where fill thickness varied from half a metre to over four metres across a single lot, because the original ground surface had been sculpted by pre-European Māori gardening and later quarrying. Seismic tomography can map these buried irregularities without the need for a dense grid of probes, flagging zones where differential settlement might compromise pavements or shallow footings. Missing a soft pocket beneath a stormwater detention basin or a cycleway bridge abutment leads to repairs that cost far more than the geophysical survey itself, measured in both dollars and reputational damage to the engineering consultancy.

Our services

The seismic tomography workflow is tailored to the specific site constraints and engineering questions in the Waikato context. Each survey design starts with a desktop review of existing logs and aerial imagery, then moves to field acquisition with real-time quality control.

Refraction Tomography for Bedrock Mapping

Ideal for setting pile refusal depths and planning rock excavation. We model the compressional-wave velocity field to delineate the soil-rock interface with sub-metre accuracy, even beneath basalt floaters.

High-Resolution Reflection Profiling

Applied to shallow stratigraphic targets such as buried channels, peat horizons, and the contact between the Hinuera Formation and underlying units. The method provides vertical resolution on the order of 0.5 m in favourable ground.

Integrated Seismic & Resistivity Cross-Sections

When groundwater or clay content affects the seismic signal, we pair P-wave tomography with electrical resistivity imaging to produce a dual-parameter geophysical model that reduces ambiguity in the interpretation.

Frequently asked questions

How deep can seismic tomography see in the Hamilton basin?

Refraction surveys with a 115-metre spread typically resolve features down to 25–35 metres below surface in Hamilton's alluvial and weak-rock conditions. Reflection profiling can reach 80–100 metres when a heavier weight-drop source is used, although resolution decreases with depth. The actual penetration depends on the velocity contrast between layers; the high-impedance contact at the top of the Te Kuiti Group limestone usually produces a strong, interpretable arrival.

Can seismic tomography detect voids or sinkholes?

Yes, seismic tomography is one of the most effective geophysical methods for locating cavities in limestone or buried voids in fill. A void produces a low-velocity anomaly that stands out against the surrounding rock velocity, and both refraction and cross-hole tomography have successfully mapped karst features in the Te Kuiti Group beneath Hamilton's northern suburbs.
