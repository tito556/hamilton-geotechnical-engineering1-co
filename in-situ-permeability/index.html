We see it often on Hamilton sites: the contractor assumes the ground is tight because the topsoil looks dry at surface level. Two metres down, the peat or pumice sand tells a completely different story, and the excavation floods within hours. A properly executed Lefranc or Lugeon test eliminates that guesswork before it becomes a site shutdown. We run these tests under NZGS guidelines across Hamilton Basin formations, from the Hinuera alluvium to the Hamilton Ash layers, delivering hydraulic conductivity values you can rely on for dewatering design or grouting verification. The data feeds directly into groundwater control plans where peat interbeds create perched water tables that surface mapping simply cannot detect.

The difference between a dry excavation and a flooded one in Hamilton usually comes down to a single peat layer the desktop study missed.

Methodology applied in Hamilton

The Hamilton Basin sits on a complex Quaternary sequence where the Hinuera Formation — predominantly coarse to medium sands with gravel lenses — alternates with layers of low-permeability Hamilton Ash and peat horizons that can completely disrupt vertical drainage assumptions. We measure this variability directly through in-situ permeability testing at depths ranging from 3 to 60 metres below ground level. The Lefranc method covers soils and weak rock where a stable borehole section can be isolated, while the Lugeon test applies to fractured rock encountered beneath the alluvium in the western margins toward Te Uku. Both procedures follow NZGS guideline protocols for falling-head and constant-head configurations, with data corrected for temperature and effective stress conditions. A single borehole typically yields four to six test intervals, each producing a discrete k-value that captures the vertical heterogeneity inherent to Waikato subsurface conditions.

Typical technical challenges in Hamilton

A commercial development on Te Rapa Road planned a two-level basement cut into what the borelog described as stiff silt. The contractor proceeded with a generic wellpoint system and hit a pressurised sand lens at 5.2 metres depth. Water inflow exceeded 40 litres per second within twenty minutes, collapsing the eastern batter and flooding the entire excavation. The job stood idle for eleven days while emergency dewatering was installed. What would have flagged the risk was a single Lugeon test in the borehole intersecting that sand, showing a sudden jump in hydraulic conductivity by two orders of magnitude. In Hamilton, where the Hinuera sands can carry significant artesian pressure beneath confining ash or peat layers, skipping field permeability measurement turns a manageable groundwater condition into a structural failure and a six-figure delay.

Common questions

What is the difference between a Lefranc test and a Lugeon test?

Lefranc tests measure hydraulic conductivity in soil or soft rock using an open section within a borehole, typically under falling-head or constant-head conditions. Lugeon tests use inflatable packers to isolate a section of borehole in harder fractured rock, applying staged water pressure to determine rock mass permeability in Lugeon units. In Hamilton, we apply Lefranc to the Hinuera sands and silts, and Lugeon where boreholes encounter basalt or greywacke at depth.

How much does a field permeability test cost in Hamilton?

The cost for a Lefranc or Lugeon test in Hamilton ranges from NZ$900 to NZ$1,510 per borehole depending on the number of test intervals, depth, and whether packer isolation is required. A full permeability profiling programme across multiple boreholes typically falls within this range per hole.

How long does a field permeability test take on site?

A single Lefranc test interval takes approximately 45 to 90 minutes depending on soil permeability and whether steady-state conditions are achieved quickly. A Lugeon test with multiple pressure stages can take two to three hours per interval. We plan testing sequences to run concurrently with other site investigation activities where possible.

Do I need field permeability tests if I already have grain size data from lab testing?

Yes, and we strongly recommend it. Grain size analysis gives an indirect estimate of permeability using empirical correlations like Hazen, but it does not account for in-situ fabric, fissures, layering, or confining stress — all of which control actual groundwater flow.
