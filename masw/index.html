What we see time and again across Hamilton is that the ground isn't what it looks like from the surface. The city sits on a mix of ancient peat swamps, recent alluvium from the Waikato River, and pockets of volcanic ash from the Taupo Volcanic Zone. That variability matters when you're trying to figure out site class. A single test pit might show firm silt at 2 metres, but it tells you nothing about what's happening at 20 or 30 metres depth. That's exactly where the shear wave velocity profile becomes critical. We run the MASW survey with a 24-channel seismograph and 4.5 Hz geophones, walking the array across the site to build a layered Vs model. The method works surprisingly well in Hamilton's suburban lots, even with the background hum from State Highway 1. The data feeds directly into VS30 calculations, and from there we assign the NZS 1170.5 site class that the structural engineer needs.

In Hamilton's peat and alluvium, a 0.5-second site period can amplify ground motion by a factor of two compared to rock. VS30 captures that risk before the foundation is designed.

Methodology applied in Hamilton

The soil contrast between Hamilton East and the western suburbs near Dinsdale illustrates why shear wave velocity testing here requires careful interpretation. In Hamilton East, you're often on the Hinuera Formation, a stiff ignimbrite-derived gravel that produces Vs values above 300 m/s within the top 10 metres. Cross the river toward Te Rapa or Rotokauri, and you hit deep peat and soft alluvial silts where Vs can sit below 150 m/s for the first 15 metres. Same city, completely different seismic response. Our active-source MASW array captures that transition sharply because we use a 48-metre spread with 2-metre geophone spacing, which gives us reliable dispersion curves down to about 25 metres of investigation depth. For deeper profiles where the bedrock contact is suspected beyond 30 metres, we combine the active survey with passive-source recordings using the linear array, picking up ambient noise to extend the curve. The processed profile then ties into a liquefaction assessment where the fines content and Vs are cross-checked against the Boulanger-Idriss (2014) triggering curves, which is standard practice for any Hamilton site with shallow groundwater and clean sands below the peat layer.

Typical technical challenges in Hamilton

Hamilton sits about 120 kilometres from the Hauraki Plains fault zone and roughly 200 kilometres from the Taupo Rift, so the seismic hazard is moderate but real. The bigger issue here is the ground amplification, not the raw peak ground acceleration. The deep Waikato basin traps seismic energy. Soft sediments that extend to 40 or 50 metres depth can resonate at periods that match the natural frequency of two-to-four-storey buildings, the kind you see going up around Rototuna and Flagstaff. We've measured site periods above 0.6 seconds on several commercial sites north of the city centre. If the VS30 profile places the site in Class D or Class E, the design spectrum changes significantly under NZS 1170.5, and that often triggers a slope stability review for any cut deeper than 2 metres on the river terrace edges. The geotechnical report then carries a recommendation for specific ground improvement or foundation deepening, depending on how the Vs profile looks across the full depth.

Our services

Every MASW survey we run in Hamilton is part of a wider site investigation package. The shear wave profile rarely stands alone; it connects to the borehole log, the SPT data, and the laboratory classification tests. The services below are what we typically bundle together for a complete seismic site characterisation.

Active-source MASW survey

24-channel array with sledgehammer source, 2m geophone spacing. Delivers 1D Vs profile and VS30 calculation for standard residential and low-rise commercial sites.

Combined active/passive MASW

Adds ambient noise recording to extend the dispersion curve beyond 30 metres. Required for sites in central Hamilton and near the river where deep soft sediments are expected.

Site class determination report

Formal letter or report section assigning NZS 1170.5 site class (A through E) with supporting Vs profile, dispersion curve, and VS30 table. Accepted by Hamilton City Council for building consent.

Liquefaction triggering integration

Combines the MASW Vs profile with CPT or SPT data to run the Boulanger-Idriss (2014) liquefaction triggering analysis, producing factor of safety and LPI plots for the site.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a MASW survey cost in Hamilton for a standard residential section?

For a single active-source MASW line on a typical 600-to-800-square-metre residential section in Hamilton, the survey fee ranges from NZ$2.390 to NZ$3.150, including data processing, dispersion analysis, and the site class determination letter. If the site requires passive-source extension to reach deeper than 30 metres, or multiple survey lines for a larger commercial lot, the total can go up to NZ$4.930. The exact cost depends on access conditions, array length, and whether we combine the survey with a borehole or CPT on the same day.

What is the difference between MASW and downhole seismic testing for shear wave velocity?

MASW is a non-invasive surface method that measures the dispersion of Rayleigh waves along a geophone array, while downhole seismic requires a borehole and a downhole geophone lowered to different depths. In Hamilton's soft sediments, MASW works particularly well because the strong velocity contrast between the alluvium and the underlying Hinuera Formation produces a clear dispersion curve. The main trade-off is that MASW gives an averaged 1D profile under the array, whereas downhole testing gives a point measurement. For most building consent applications, MASW is accepted by Hamilton City Council as the primary Vs measurement method.

How long does a MASW survey take on site, and when do we get the results?

The field work for a single active-source MASW line takes about two hours on a Hamilton residential site, including array layout, calibration shots, and recording. If we add passive-source recording, budget another 30 to 40 minutes of ambient noise capture. The data processing and inversion back at the office takes one to two working days. The final site class report with the Vs profile, VS30 value, and NZS 1170.5 classification is typically delivered within three working days of the field survey.
